Hospitalised Injuries from Road Crashes in Australia

Hospitalised road crash injuries changed over time in Australia, but the burden is not equal across all groups. This visualisation guides you through who is most affected, where injuries happen, how the sex gap differs by road user, and what crash interactions are involved.

Section 01

How did hospitalised injuries change from 2011 to 2021?

Hospitalised road crash injuries rose 16% nationally between 2011 and 2021. But the increase was not equal across road users or geographic areas. Use the filters below to explore where the change is concentrated.

Hospitalised injuries increased overall between 2011 and 2021, but the growth was not equal across all road users and areas. The road user filter shows that some groups, especially pedal cyclists, experienced stronger growth than the national pattern. The remoteness filter helps users compare whether the injury burden is concentrated in major cities, regional areas, or remote areas.

Section 02

Which road users carry the largest share of injuries?

Different road users contribute different proportions of the total injury burden, and the picture changes dramatically when you separate males from females.

This chart shows how the hospitalised injury burden is distributed across road user groups. Car drivers generally form a large share, but the pattern changes when the sex filter is used. Male hospitalisations show a stronger motorcycle-related burden, while female hospitalisations place relatively more weight on car drivers and passengers. This helps users compare injury share, not just total counts.

Section 03

Which age groups are most affected?

Road crashes are often assumed to be a young person's problem. The data tells a more complicated story.

The age profile shows that hospitalised road crash injuries are not only concentrated among young people. In many filtered views, middle-aged adults form a large part of the injury burden, especially the 40–64 age group. The road user filter helps show whether age patterns differ between drivers, passengers, motorcyclists, cyclists, and pedestrians.

Section 04

How do different road users actually get injured?

Hospitalisation counts tell us who is affected, but not how. The crash counterparty — what the injured person collided with or was involved with — reveals very different patterns for different road users.

Crash interaction patterns differ strongly by road user. Pedestrian injuries are more often linked with car and van collisions, while cyclists and motorcyclists show more non-collision or solo events. This suggests road safety responses should consider how each group is injured. Pedestrian safety may focus on vehicle interaction, while cyclist and motorcyclist safety may also require attention to road surface, infrastructure design, speed, and loss-of-control incidents.

Key Findings

  • Hospitalised road crash injuries increased between 2011 and 2021, but the increase was not equal across all road users.
  • Road user type is important for understanding the injury burden, as drivers, passengers, motorcyclists, cyclists, and pedestrians show different patterns.
  • Sex-based filtering shows that some road user groups, especially motorcyclists, have a much stronger male injury burden.
  • Age-group analysis shows that hospitalised injuries are not only a young-person issue; middle-aged and older groups also carry a significant burden.
  • Crash interaction patterns differ by road user, meaning safety responses should consider how injuries happen, not only who is injured.

Data Source: Bureau of Infrastructure and Transport Research Economics (BITRE), Hospitalised Injury Publication, September 2023. National hospitalised injuries from road traffic crashes, 2011–2021. Raw data processed and aggregated in KNIME.
